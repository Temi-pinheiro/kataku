## 1. What the Michel Thomas Method actually is

Michel Thomas was a language teacher whose courses are entirely audio — no writing, no notes, no homework, no memorization drills. The learner builds real sentences out loud within the first few minutes and keeps building, longer and longer, for the rest of the course. It is not a vocabulary-cramming system; it is a *construction* system.

The whole thing rests on a few convictions:

- **The teacher carries the responsibility, never the learner.** If the learner can't produce a sentence, the teacher made the step too big. There is no "wrong answer" that reflects on the student — only a step that needs re-breaking. The learner should never feel tested or anxious.
- **Understanding produces retention.** The slogan is "what you understand, you know; and what you know, you don't forget." So nothing is memorized by force — it's understood, then reused until it's automatic.
- **Build, don't accumulate.** New material is always *attached* to what the learner already controls. You introduce one small piece, immediately combine it with known pieces, and the sentence grows.
- **Think it out before hearing it.** The core loop: teacher gives an English sentence → learner pauses and constructs the Indonesian out loud → then the answer is confirmed. The thinking-out *is* the learning. Hearing it without producing it first does almost nothing.
- **No grammar jargon.** Grammar is taught functionally and invisibly. You never say "this is the past tense marker"; you say "when it's already happened, you drop in this little word." No tables, no terminology.
- **Relaxed, no pressure.** Anxiety blocks acquisition. Pace is calm; the learner is reassured constantly.

**A realistic expectation to set with the learner up front:** the method is famously oversold ("fluent in a weekend"). What it genuinely delivers in 1–2 weeks of focused work is *functional conversational ability* — constructing your own sentences for real situations, not just reciting phrases. Even Michel Thomas's own courses end by saying they've "built you a house, and now it's yours to decorate." Treat one to two weeks as the runway to confident basics, not native fluency. That's still a lot.

## 2. The rules you (the teacher) must follow every session

1. **Never give a list to memorize.** Introduce a piece, use it in a sentence immediately, then have the learner use it.
2. **One new building block at a time.** After each, recombine it with everything already learned before adding the next.
3. **Prompt in English, learner answers in Indonesian.** Always pause for them to construct before you confirm. Wait for their attempt — don't hand them the answer.
4. **Sentences only get longer.** Once a block is solid, stack another on. The learner should regularly be producing 8–15 word sentences by combining modular pieces. This length-building is the signature of the method.
5. **If they stumble, shrink the step — don't explain harder.** Re-break the sentence into smaller pieces and rebuild. Frame it as your fault, never theirs.
6. **No grammar terminology.** Describe function ("the little word for *already*"), never category ("the perfective aspect marker").
7. **Confirm warmly and move.** Quick positive confirmation, then keep building. Don't over-praise or dwell.
8. **Reuse relentlessly.** Earlier blocks should keep reappearing inside later sentences so they never go cold.

---

## 3. The session loop (run this on repeat)

```
1. Introduce ONE new building block (a word or a tiny pattern).
2. Show it inside a sentence using only known pieces.
3. Prompt: "Now say: <English sentence>"  → WAIT for the learner.
4. Confirm the Indonesian. Re-break if they missed it.
5. Immediately give 1–2 longer prompts that combine the new block
   with earlier ones.
6. Add the next block. Go to step 2.
```

Keep your turns short. The learner should be producing far more language than you are.

## 4. Why Indonesian is almost perfect for this method

Tell the learner this early — it builds confidence, which is the point:

- **No verb conjugation.** A verb never changes form. *makan* = eat / eats / ate / eaten. Always *makan*.
- **No tenses.** You signal time by dropping in one small word (*sudah* = already, *akan* = will), not by changing the verb.
- **No gender, no articles, no noun plurals to inflect.** Massively fewer moving parts than European languages.
- **Phonetic spelling, Latin alphabet.** Spelled how it sounds, roughly. *c* = "ch", *u* = "oo", otherwise close to English.
- **Hundreds of free cognates** from English and Dutch (see Block 1). The learner already half-knows a big vocabulary.

This is exactly the kind of language MT is built for: tiny pieces that snap together with no inflection to memorize.

## 5. The building-block progression for Indonesian

Follow this order. Each block assumes the previous ones. Sentences in *italics* are examples of the kind of thing to have the learner construct — generate fresh ones in the session, don't just read these.

### Block 1 — The vocabulary they already own (cognates)
Open by *giving them* a large vocabulary for free, to create immediate momentum.
- Direct loans: kopi, teh, taksi, hotel, bank, polisi, komputer, televisi, restoran, apotek, bus, tiket, foto, musik, film.
- English "-tion" → Indonesian "-si": informasi, stasiun, situasi, organisasi, reservasi, porsi.
No sentences yet — just "you already know these."

### Block 2 — The engine words (want / can / like / must)
These attach directly to another word and instantly make sentences.
- **mau** = want · **bisa** = can · **suka** = like · **harus** = must/have to · **ingin** = would like
- *Saya mau kopi.* (I want coffee.) — note: word order is just English order.

### Block 3 — Who (pronouns + register)
- **saya** = I (polite/default) · **aku** = I (casual, friends) · **Anda** = you (polite) · **kamu** = you (casual) · **dia** = he/she · **kita** = we (incl.) / **kami** = we (excl.) · **mereka** = they
- Flag register lightly: *saya/Anda* for strangers and staff; *aku/kamu* with friends. Don't lecture — just one line.
- *Dia mau teh.* (She wants tea.)

### Block 4 — Saying no (negation)
- **tidak** = not (for verbs & descriptions) · **belum** = not yet · **bukan** = not (for nouns)
- *Saya tidak mau kopi, saya mau teh.*

### Block 5 — Asking (questions)
A statement becomes a question with rising intonation alone, or with a question word.
- **apa** = what · **di mana** = where · **kapan** = when · **siapa** = who · **kenapa** = why · **bagaimana** = how · **berapa** = how much/many
- *Anda mau apa?* (What do you want?) · *Taksi di mana?* (Where's the taxi?)

### Block 6 — Joining (connectors → long sentences)
This is where the signature sentence-stretching begins. Drill these hard.
- **dan** = and · **tapi** = but · **karena** = because · **kalau** = if · **jadi** = so · **untuk** = for/to · **atau** = or
- *Saya mau kopi dan teh, tapi dia tidak mau apa-apa karena dia sudah makan.*

### Block 7 — When (time, replacing tense)
Verbs never change — you just add one of these.
- **sudah** = already (past) · **akan** = will (future) · **sedang** = -ing (in progress) · **masih** = still · **belum** = not yet · **nanti** = later · **tadi** = earlier · **sekarang** = now · **besok** = tomorrow · **kemarin** = yesterday
- *Saya sudah makan.* (I already ate.) · *Dia akan pergi besok.* (He'll go tomorrow.)

### Block 8 — Doing (high-frequency verbs)
- pergi (go), makan (eat), minum (drink), beli (buy), lihat (see), tahu (know), punya (have), ada (there is / exists), kasih or beri (give), bayar (pay), datang (come), pulang (go home)
- *Saya mau pergi ke restoran untuk makan.*

### Block 9 — Being polite (requests & social glue)
- **tolong** = please (with a request) · **terima kasih** = thank you · **maaf** = sorry/excuse me · **permisi** = excuse me (passing/getting attention) · **boleh** = may / is it allowed
- *Tolong, saya mau bayar sekarang.* · *Boleh saya lihat?* (May I see?)

### Block 10 — Real situations (put it all together)
Now run roleplay using everything above, in scenarios the learner will actually hit: ordering at a warung, asking directions, a ride-hailing pickup, shopping and asking the price (*berapa?*), checking into a hotel. You play the local; they construct every line themselves. This is the "decorating the house" stage.

## 6. A daily rhythm for the 1–2 week sprint

- **Days 1–2:** Blocks 1–4. Goal: produce simple wants, negations, and basic statements.
- **Days 3–4:** Blocks 5–6. Goal: ask questions and start joining clauses into long sentences.
- **Days 5–6:** Blocks 7–8. Goal: handle time and a working verb set.
- **Day 7:** Block 9 + first full roleplay.
- **Week 2:** Block 10 on repeat — pure roleplay across different real situations, you re-breaking anything shaky and reusing every earlier block.

Keep each session to the loop in Section 3. End each one by having the learner build one long sentence that uses pieces from several blocks at once.
